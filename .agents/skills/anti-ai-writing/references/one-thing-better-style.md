# One Thing Better Writing Style

*Decision Frameworks and Risk Reframing*

## Core Philosophy

The One Thing Better voice uses framework-driven narratives to help readers make better decisions. It teaches readers HOW TO THINK about decisions and tradeoffs, not just WHAT to decide.

**Essence:** Every decision has risks on both sides—risks of action AND risks of inaction. Once you see that clearly, you become empowered to choose.

**Core principle:** You're not just informing readers; you're teaching them a repeatable thinking pattern they can apply to their own decisions.

---

## Fundamental Insight: Every Decision Has Two Costs

### The Problem with How We Think About Decisions

When we decide whether to do something, we ask ourselves two questions:
1. What's the upside to doing this?
2. What's the downside to doing this?

**But there's something missing:** What happens if you don't do that thing?

### The Solution: Risk of Action + Risk of Inaction

There are risks of action AND risks of inaction. Every decision has a cost—either because of what we do, or what we do not do.

**You cannot truly know the wisdom of a decision—and therefore decide whether to make that decision—until you explore the costs of not making the decision.**

### The Framework

When facing a decision, always ask:

**If I do nothing:** What happens? What are the actual consequences?

**If I do something:** What happens? What are the real consequences?

Then compare: Which scenario has the better potential outcome? Which risks are you better equipped to handle?

## How This Works: The Real Example

### The Scenario: Steve's Dilemma

Steve is a former prison inmate trying to turn his life around. He landed a full-time job offer after freelance work with a major company. His supervisor said: "Just fill these forms out. We'll do a background check, and then let's get to work."

Steve panicked. A background check would reveal his conviction. Should he tell his supervisor about it first?

### Analyzing the Risks

**If Steve does nothing:**
- The company will run a background check
- They'll discover his conviction
- Company executives discuss it privately
- Steve doesn't know what they're thinking
- They might wonder why he didn't tell them
- They might assume he tried to hide it

**If Steve does something:**
- Steve tells his manager before the background check
- He explains his crime, punishment, and efforts to move forward
- His manager hears his story directly
- Steve owns his narrative
- He shows he has nothing to hide

### The Key Insight

In both scenarios, the company will find out about his conviction. The only difference is HOW they find out and what story gets told.

**The risk of inaction = Company discovers it secretly + wonders about hidden motives**
**The risk of action = Company learns from Steve directly + Steve controls the narrative**

### The Decision

After going through this exercise, Steve decided to be upfront. His supervisor thanked him, said he was brave, and promised he'd still get the job.

Because Steve took action, he now has the opportunity he wanted and can restart his life.

## Key Stylistic Elements

### 1. Personal Story as Framework Illustration

**What it is:** Using a real person's story to make an abstract framework concrete.

**How to use:**
- Introduce a character and their dilemma
- Show the false framework people usually use
- Introduce the true framework (costs of action AND inaction)
- Apply the framework to the character's story
- Generalize back to reader

**Why it works:** Stories stick. Frameworks are abstract. Together, they're powerful.

### 2. Risk Analysis (Both Sides)

**What it is:** Systematically examining what could go wrong if you act, and what could go wrong if you don't.

**How to use:**
- State both scenarios clearly
- What's the risk of action?
- What's the risk of inaction?
- Which risks are acceptable?
- Which can you live with?

**Never:** Frame one side as obviously better without showing both costs

### 3. The Reframing Move

**What it is:** Showing how the same situation looks different when you change your perspective.

**Example:**
- **Old frame:** "Either I get this job or I don't"
- **New frame:** "Either I tell my truth and control the story, or I hide and hope they don't find out"

**How it works:** Changes what the decision is really about

### 4. Teaching a Thinking Pattern

**What it is:** The reader should walk away with a framework they can apply to other decisions.

**How to achieve:**
- Explicitly name the framework ("Every decision has costs on both sides")
- Show it applied to the example
- Show how readers could apply it themselves
- Give them the questions to ask

**Application examples:**
- Making a career change
- Having a difficult conversation
- Speaking up about something
- Making an unpopular choice

## Decision-Making Framework

### Step 1: Name the Decision
What are you actually deciding between?
- Not "Should I do X?" but "Do I tell the truth or hide it?"
- Not "Should I change jobs?" but "Do I stay comfortable or risk growth?"

### Step 2: Identify the False Framework
What's the common way people think about this decision?
- Usually oversimplified
- Usually ignores costs of inaction
- Usually creates false binary

### Step 3: Introduce the True Framework
- Costs of action (real, specific)
- Costs of inaction (real, specific)
- Neither side is safe
- Question: Which risks can you actually live with?

### Step 4: Apply to the Specific Situation
- Use concrete details
- Show the character thinking through both sides
- Show them choosing based on the framework
- Show the results

### Step 5: Generalize Back to Reader
- How could readers use this framework?
- What other decisions could it apply to?
- What questions should they ask themselves?

## The Reframing Principle

### Common False Frames
- "Action is risky, inaction is safe" ← FALSE
- "I should avoid all risk" ← IMPOSSIBLE
- "The safest choice is to do nothing" ← OFTEN BACKWARDS
- "Some options have no risks" ← FANTASY

### The Truth
- **Both action and inaction have risks**
- **You cannot avoid risk, only choose which risks**
- **Doing nothing is also a decision with consequences**
- **Empowerment comes from seeing both sides clearly**

---

## How to Apply This Style

### 1. Start with a Real Dilemma
- Use a person facing a genuine decision
- Make their stakes clear
- Show their fear or hesitation

### 2. Show the False Framework
- How are they currently thinking about it?
- What assumption are they making?
- Why is it incomplete?

### 3. Introduce the True Framework
- Both action and inaction have costs
- Which costs can they actually live with?
- Which scenario has the better potential outcome?

### 4. Apply Systematically
- "If you do nothing: X, Y, Z"
- "If you do something: A, B, C"
- Show the actual consequences

### 5. Make the Decision Clear
- Not what they should do (that's their choice)
- But what becomes possible once they see both sides
- How empowerment comes from clarity

### 6. Generalize the Pattern
- What's the framework?
- How could readers apply it?
- What decisions is this useful for?

## Voice Consistency Checklist

### Before Publishing, Ask:

- [ ] **Is there a real person with a real dilemma?**
- [ ] **Do I show the false framework people usually use?**
- [ ] **Do I clearly lay out risks of BOTH action and inaction?**
- [ ] **Is the reframe genuine, not manipulative?**
- [ ] **Can readers apply this framework to their own decisions?**
- [ ] **Do I respect that they might choose differently?**
- [ ] **Is the story specific enough to feel real?**
- [ ] **Does the pattern generalize beyond the specific case?**

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

### 1. Being Manipulative
**Bad:** Using the framework to push a specific decision
**Good:** Using the framework to clarify thinking, respecting their choice

### 2. Oversimplifying
**Bad:** Pretending hard decisions are easy once you "see" the framework
**Good:** Acknowledging that both sides have real costs

### 3. Using Generic Examples
**Bad:** Abstract dilemmas that don't feel real
**Good:** Specific, detailed stories that ground the framework

### 4. Missing the Generalization
**Bad:** Teaching a lesson that only applies to one situation
**Good:** Teaching a thinking pattern that's broadly applicable

### 5. False Binaries
**Bad:** "Either you do this or that happens"
**Good:** "Here are the real tradeoffs"

## Real-World Applications

### Career Decisions
- **Old frame:** "Should I change jobs?"
- **New frame:** "What are the risks of staying? What are the risks of leaving?"

### Difficult Conversations
- **Old frame:** "Should I tell them?"
- **New frame:** "What's the risk if I speak up? What's the risk if I stay silent?"

### Parenting Decisions
- **Old frame:** "What's the best choice for my child?"
- **New frame:** "What are the costs of this approach? What are the costs of the alternative?"

### Educational Choices
- **Old frame:** "Should we try a different school?"
- **New frame:** "What risks do we take by staying? What risks by changing?"

---

## Key Takeaway

The One Thing Better style works because it:

1. **Teaches thinking, not answers** - Readers walk away with a framework, not just a story
2. **Honors complexity** - Acknowledges both action and inaction have costs
3. **Empowers readers** - Shows how clarity about tradeoffs leads to better decisions
4. **Uses stories effectively** - Makes frameworks concrete and memorable
5. **Is broadly applicable** - The same thinking pattern works across different contexts

Use this style when your goal is to help readers think better about decisions, not just understand a specific situation. It's most powerful for readers facing genuine dilemmas where clarity about tradeoffs is more valuable than a prescribed answer.
